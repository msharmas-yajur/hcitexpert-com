Care that is delivered is often not important1.</b></span></blockquote><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The importance of clinical care grounded in a reliable evidence base cannot be over-emphasised. </span></span><span>Evidence-based care processes, supported by automated clinical information and decision support systems, </span><span>offer the greatest promise of achieving the best outcomes2. Proprietary Clinical Decision Support Systems </span><span>(CDSS) built on evidence-adaptive platforms incorporating clinical knowledge that continually reflects </span><span>current EBM gleaned from both the research literature and sources of practice expertise will soon outgrow </span><span>self-synthesised (home-grown) solutions. This paper explores this process.</span><br /><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Clinical practice is full of contradictions, not only where individual </span></span><span>professional experiences conflict, but even where “evidence” partially or </span><span>completely disagrees. The primary reason for these inconsistencies is </span><span>that evidence is dynamic and emergent, never constant.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span>The Fallibility of Evidence</span></h1><div style="text-align:start;"><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"></div></div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Evidence can often be incomplete, with varying levels of quality and strength of recommendations3. </span></span><span>Keeping up with latest evidence and eliminating its inconsistencies is quite an arduous task and carries </span><span>the inherent risk of practicing outdated medicine (with occasional catastrophic consequences). </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Consider the following scenario:</span><br /><span><br /></span> <i><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">A 2 month-old infant comes to your office suffering from heart failure. She has a prescription for </span></span><span>two drugs that reduce excess fluids from the body (diuretics), prescribed by a cardiologist based on </span><span>evidence demonstrating the effectiveness of the two medications when administered together. </span><span>One of the drugs reduces the body’s level of potassium (an important electrolyte) while the other </span><span>conserves potassium. You are doubtful that two drugs are required for the treatment of such a </span><span>young patient. Given the amount of time it will take to find evidence to address your skepticism, </span><span>you call the cardiologist. Unfortunately, the prescribing cardiologist is unavailable, so you then call </span><span>another renowned cardiologist. He tells you to stop the second drug based on professional </span><span>experience that it causes growth problems in infants as well as his belief that potassium loss is of </span><span>little concern in infants. You are now left wondering what is best for your tiny patient, having </span><span>moved from a stage of having no information to a stage of conflicting “information noise.”</span></i><br /><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Given such realities of evidence-based medicine, one must consider: is the business of extrapolating </span></span><span>evidence something providers and healthcare organisations are willing to do on their own?</span><br /><span><br /></span> <span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span>THE EVIDENCE DELUGE</span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">It has been estimated that greater than two million articles are published in the biomedical literature each </span></span><span>year4. If a physician were to attempt to keep up with this literary explosion by reading two articles each day, </span><span>at the end of one year, that physician would be more than sixty centuries behind! If physicians were to read </span><span>everything of possible clinical relevance, they would need to read around 6,000 articles a day! </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Compounding this problem is the conundrum of diffusion. “Diffusion” is the spread of best (research) </span><span>evidence on managing diseases and symptoms to the patient bedside5. According to conventional wisdom, </span><span>it takes an average of 17 years for validated clinical research findings to make their way into routine </span><span>clinical practice6. In an age where global public health emergencies (like the recent Zika virus outbreak) </span><span>require “knowledge hyper-loops” for rapid diffusion of knowledge into general practice, the 17-year latency </span><span>needs to be radically shortened to 17 hours or even less.</span><br /><span><br /></span> <br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span>A SOLUTION TO ACCELERATE LEARNING HEALTH SYSTEMS</span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS) have been described as the Computerised Patient Record (CPR) </span></span><span>System’s Crown Jewel7. According to Gartner’s CPR generations (Fig 1), CPRs or Electronic Health Records </span><span>(EHRs) have had an increasingly positive impact over the last few decades in reducing medical errors. With </span><span>the inclusion of CDSS, the EHR evolves from being a provider “colleague” to a “mentor,” with the power to </span><span>cover the entire care continuum in guiding clinicians at all points of care.</span><br /><span><br /></span> <br /><div class="separator" style="clear:both;text-align:center;"><a href="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/dd9d3-gartnerehrgenerations.png" style="margin-left:1em;margin-right:1em;"><img border="0" height="390" src="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/335e7-gartnerehrgenerations.png" width="640" /></a></div><span><br /></span> <span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">We are now seeing the evolution of the Sixth Generation EHR - “The Seer,” that has computable, </span></span><span>standardised clinical data able to invoke clinical decision support from evidence-adaptive CDSS platforms. </span><span>Although at present evidence-adaptive platforms require human intervention, we are now beginning to see </span><span>the inclusion of artificial neural networks (deep learning), Bayesian networks, reinforcement learning, and </span><span>other artificial intelligence techniques for synthesising evidence relevant to patient data in real-time, with </span><span>potentially unprecedented insights for clinicians. Intelligence Augmentation (IA), where technology </span><span>amplifies the decision-making capabilities of humans, has linked healthcare providers to vast amounts of </span><span>patient data with relevant clinical knowledge, in real-time, at the point-of-care. We are likely to soon </span><span>witness wide-scale proliferation of IA in Sixth Generation EHRs that incorporate evidence-adaptive CDSS.</span><span> </span><br /><span><br /></span> <br /><blockquote class="tr_bq"><i><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">This kind of evidence-adaptive CDSS is at the heart of a Learning Health </span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">System (LHS)1, wherein evidence influences practice and the practice, in turn, </span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">generates evidence, creating self-propagating, virtuous cycles that bring </span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">about better, safer clinical care at optimal costs.</span></span></i></blockquote><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><span></span></span><br /><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">There are six critical success factors (Table 1) for a CDSS, based on the ACUDIR model (Latin for “Come to </span></span><span>the Rescue”), that can form the foundation of such a rapid LHS.</span><br /><div class="separator" style="clear:both;text-align:center;"><a href="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/1ddea-acudirtable.png" style="margin-left:1em;margin-right:1em;"><img border="0" height="258" src="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/5b8f3-acudirtable.png" width="640" /></a></div><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">CDSS solutions like Order Sets, Care Plans, and Clinical Pathways are a combination of evidence-based </span></span><span>content and advanced technology platforms.
