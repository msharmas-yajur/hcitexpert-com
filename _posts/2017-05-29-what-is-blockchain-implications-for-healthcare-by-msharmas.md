In healthcare the need for presenting the Information to the Right Person at the Right Time has been proven to improve outcomes in patient treatment.</span><span><br /></span><span></span></i></blockquote><br /><blockquote class="tr_bq" style="text-align:justify;"><i><span>Will HIE 2.0 benefit from the use of Blockchain in presenting the information to the Right Person at the Right Time? </span></i></blockquote><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-2a068dbc-fe6e-999c-4605-22a11f0a3f6b" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><hr /></div><div dir="ltr" style="margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><div style="line-height:2.0736;"><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;text-align:justify;"><br /><span>What is Blockchain?</span></div><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Various definitions of Blockchain have been put across based on the context of the use. Some of these definitions are: </span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i>A digital ledger in which transactions made in bitcoin or another cryptocurrency are recorded chronologically and publicly.</i></b></span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i><br /></i></b></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i>“The blockchain is an incorruptible digital ledger of economic transactions that can be programmed to record not just financial transactions but virtually everything of value.” Don &amp; Alex Tapscott, authors Blockchain Revolution (2016)</i></b></span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i><br /></i></b></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i>The Blockchain is a decentralized ledger of all transactions across a peer-to-peer network. Using this technology, participants can confirm transactions without the need for a central certifying authority. Potential applications include, fund transfers, settling trades, voting etc.</i></b></span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i><br /></i></b></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i>Blockchain is a distributed system for recording and storing transaction records. More specifically, blockchain is a shared, immutable record of peer-to-peer transactions built from linked transaction blocks and stored in a digital ledger. [1]</i></b></span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i><br /></i></b></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b><i><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">A Blockchain is a data structure that can be timed-stamped and signed using a private key to prevent tampering. There are generally three types of Blockchain: public, private and consortium. [6]</span></span><span> </span></i></b></span></span><br /><br /><a name='more'></a><br /><br /><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="color:black;font-family:Times;font-size:medium;font-style:normal;font-variant-caps:normal;font-variant-ligatures:normal;font-weight:normal;letter-spacing:normal;line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;orphans:2;text-align:justify;text-decoration-color:initial;text-decoration-style:initial;text-indent:0;text-transform:none;white-space:normal;widows:2;word-spacing:0;"><div style="margin:0;"><span>How is Blockchain different?</span></div></div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Traditional databases are proprietary to the entity that maintains them and owns them. And the information stored within these databases are accessed only by providing access via an application or shared by the entity in some form of a distributed architecture. </span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">On the other hand, "blockchain is enabling a database to be directly shared across boundaries of trust, without requiring a central administrator. This is possible because blockchain transactions contain their own proof of validity and their own proof of authorization, instead of requiring some centralized application logic to enforce those constraints. Transactions can therefore be verified and processed independently by multiple “nodes”, with the blockchain acting as a consensus mechanism to ensure those nodes stay in sync." [2]</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">A quite often stated example for explaining Blockchain is the Google Doc example. Earlier, collaborating on a document involved a serial approach to making changes to a document. Only once the author has completed the document, can it be forwarded to the next person to edit and provide feedback. </span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">But consider the Google Doc (or any of the other collaboration tools), once you have created a google doc, you can start creating the document and also share the same document with other collaborators who can also make changes to the document at the same time allowing for reconciliation of changes to be incorporated within the document to finalise it. The author takes the comments from the collaborators and generates the finalised document.</span></span><span> </span></span></span><span> </span><br /><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;"><span><br /></span><span>Blockchain: How it Works?</span></div><span>A transaction is requested. The transaction is broadcasted to the peer-to-peer network consisting of computer nodes. The network validates the transaction and the initiating entity's status using relevant algorithms.  The transaction record is then considered to be verified.</span><br /><span><br /></span><span>On verification, the transaction record is added with other transactions to create a new block of data for the </span><i>decentralized ledger of all transactions across a peer-to-peer network.</i><br /><i><br /></i><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The new Block is added to the existing ledger of all transactions, i.e., the Blockchain. The transaction is now complete.</span></span><span> </span><br /><i><br /></i>  <br /><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;"><span>Types of Blockchains</span><br /><div><span><b><i>Permissionless or Unpermissioned</i></b> Blockchain allows anyone to join the network and participate in the block verification. For instance, a permissionless blockchain example is the Bitcoin.</span></div><div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><span><b><i>Permissioned</i></b> Blockchains restricts the nodes in the network who can contribute to the consensus of the system. Only permissioned nodes have the rights to validate the block transactions.</span></div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><span>For instance, most enterprise Blockchains are <b><i>permissioned blockchain</i></b> and <b><i>allow for privacy, scalability and fine-grained access control</i></b>. [5]</span><br /><span><br /></span><span>There are more types of Blockchains.</span></div><div><br /></div></div></div><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;"><span>Interoperability in Healthcare</span></div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The context of discussing Blockchains in healthcare is Interoperability. There are various use cases that come to mind, when we talk about interoperability in Healthcare. And that allows one to understand the current fragmented nature of the Patient's Healthcare Information. </span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Each of the above stakeholders, generate the patient care record and have the need at one time or another to share this information with others in the ecosystem. We have already seen the benefits and barriers to information exchange. </span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">For the purpose of this blog, lets consider the Healthcare Information exchange use case.
