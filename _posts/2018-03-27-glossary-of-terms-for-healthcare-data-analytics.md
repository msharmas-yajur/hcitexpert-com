These are service user perspective, internal management perspective, continuous improvement perspective and financial perspective.</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"></span></span><br /><a name='more'></a><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">BENCHMARK:</span></span></h1><span>A point of reference or standard by which something can be measured</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">BENCHMARKING:</span></span></h1><div><span>The process of comparing the cost, cycle time, productivity, or quality of a specific process or method to another that is widely considered to be an industry standard or best practice.</span></div><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">CASEMIX:</span></span></h1><span>Casemix is an internationally recognised system of measuring clinical activity incorporating the age, gender and health status of the population served by an organisation with a view to objective determination of hospital reimbursement.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">DATA:</span></span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Data are numbers, symbols, words, images, graphics that have yet to be organised or analysed</span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">DATA DICTIONARY:</span></span></h1><span>A descriptive list of names (also called representations or displays), definitions, and attributes of data elements to be collected in an information system or database.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">DATA ELEMENT:</span></span></h1><span>A unit of data for which the definition, identification, representation, and permissible values are</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">DOMAINS OF QUALITY:</span></span></h1><span>Are those definable, preferably measurable and actionable, attributes of the system that are related to its functioning to maintain, restore or improve health</span><br /><span><br /></span><span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">DESCRIPTIVE ANALYTICS</span></span></h1><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;font-size:18.6667px;">As per <a href="https://www.gartner.com/it-glossary/descriptive-analytics/" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">gartner</a>, Descriptive Analytics is the examination of data or content, usually manually performed, to answer the question “What happened?” (or What is happening?), characterized by traditional business intelligence (BI) and visualizations such as pie charts, bar charts, line graphs, tables, or generated narratives.</span><br /><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">PREDICTIVE ANALYTICS</span></span></h1><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">As per <a href="https://www.gartner.com/it-glossary/predictive-analytics" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">gartner</a>, Predictive analytics describes any approach to data mining with four attributes:</span></span><br /><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">1. An emphasis on prediction (rather than description, classification or clustering)</span></span><br /><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">2. Rapid analysis measured in hours or days (rather than the stereotypical months of traditional data mining)</span></span><br /><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">3. An emphasis on the business relevance of the resulting insights (no ivory tower analyses)</span></span><br /><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"></span></span><br /><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">4. (increasingly) An emphasis on ease of use, thus making the tools accessible to business users.</span></span><br /><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">PRESCRIPTIVE ANALYTICS</span></span></h1><span style="color:#695d46;font-family:pt serif;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">As per <a href="https://www.gartner.com/it-glossary/prescriptive-analytics" rel="nofollow noopener noreferrer" target="_blank">Gartner</a>, Prescriptive Analytics is a form of advanced analytics which examines data or content to answer the question “What should be done?” or “What can we do to make _______ happen?”, and is characterized by techniques such as graph analysis, simulation, complex event processing, neural networks, recommendation engines, heuristics, and machine learning.</span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">HEALTH INFORMATION:</span></span></h1><span>Health Information is defined as information, recorded in any form or medium, which is created or communicated by an organisation or individual relating to the past, present or future, physical or mental health or social care of an individual or cohort. It also includes information relating to the management of the health and social care system</span><br /><span><br /></span><span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">KPI SELECTION CRITERIA</span></span></h1><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"></span></span><br /><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">KPIs should be chosen based on the judgement and consensus of experts and potential <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">users.</span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"> The List of characteristics and related questions which can be </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">used to assist in the identification of KPIs. Adapted from criteria developed by the World </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Health Organization (WHO)</span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">. </span></span></span></div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><div><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></div></span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b>Validity </b></span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Does the KPI measure what it is supposed to measure? </span></span><span>A valid KPI measures what it is supposed to measure and captures an important aspect of </span><span>quality that can be influenced by the healthcare facility or system. Ideally KPIs selected </span><span>should have links to processes and outcomes through scientific evidence. Measures that </span><span>have been selected using scientific evidence possess high content validity and measures </span><span>selected through consensus and guidelines will have high face validity. </span><span>Content validity refers to whether the KPI captures important aspects of the quality of care </span><span>provided. Face validity can be determined by the KPI making sense logically and clinically or </span><span>from previous usage.</span></div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b>Reliability</b> </span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Does the KPI provide a consistent measure? </span></span><span>The KPI should provide a consistent measure in the same population and settings </span><span>irrespective of who performs the measurement. </span><span>Reliability is similar to reproducibility to the extent that if the measure is repeated you </span><span>should get the same result. Any variations in the result of the KPI should reflect actual </span><span>changes in the process or outcome. Reliability can be influenced by training, the KPI </span><span>definition and the precision of the data collection methods. </span><span>Inter-rater reliability compares differences between evaluators performing the same </span><span>measurement. Internal consistency examines the relationship between sub-indicators of the </span><span>same overall measurement, and, if reliable, there should be correlation of the results. Test-retest </span><span>reliability compares the difference between results when the same evaluator </span><span>performs the measurement at different times. </span></div></div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><div><b>Explicit evidence <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">base</span></b></div><div>Is the KPI supported by scientific evidence or the consensus of</div><div>experts? <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">KPIs should be based on scientific evidence, the consensus of expert opinions among </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">health professionals or on clinical guidelines. </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The preferred method of choosing KPIs is through evaluating scientific evidence in support </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">of each KPI and rating the strength of that evidence. One example of a rating system is to </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">give the highest rating to evidence (“A” evidence) from meta-analysis of randomised </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">controlled trials and give a lesser rating (“B” evidence) to evidence for controlled studies </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">without randomisation and a further lower rating (“C” evidence) to data from </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">epidemiological studies. </span></div><div><br /></div><div>In healthcare, there may only be limited scientific evidence to support a KPI and it becomes  Guidance on developing Key Performance Indicators and Minimum Data Sets to Monitor Healthcare Quality Health Information and Quality Authority 33 necessary to avail of expert opinion. There are a number of methods by which a KPI can be developed through facilitating group consensus from a panel of experts, such as the Delphi technique, the RAND appropriateness method and from clinical guidelines. Appendix 2 gives a brief description of each method and Appendix 3 provides an example of a Delphi assessment instrument. The expert panel can exist independently of the advisory group and are used as a point of reference for the KPI development process</div><div><br /></div><div><b>Acceptability </b></div><div>Are the KPIs acceptable? The data collected should be acceptable to those being assessed and to those carrying out the assessment. </div><div><br /></div><div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b>Feasibility</b> </span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Is it possible to collect the required data and is it worth the </span></span><span>resources? </span><span>There should be a feasibility analysis carried out to determine what data are currently </span><span>collected and the resources required to collect any additional required data. </span><span>The feasibility analysis should determine what data sources are currently available and if </span><span>they are relevant to the needs of the current project. This will include determining if there </span><span>are existing KPIs or benchmarking processes based on these data sources.</span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The reporting burden of collecting the data contained in the KPI should not outweigh the </span></span><span>value of the information obtained. Preferably, data should be integrated into service delivery, </span><span>and, where additional data are required that are not currently part of service </span><span>delivery, there should be cost benefit analysis to determine if it is cost-effective to collect.</span></div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The feasibility analysis should also include what means are used to collect data and the </span></span><span>limitations of the systems used for collection. It should also outline the reporting </span><span>arrangements, including reporting arrangements for existing data collection and frequency </span><span>of data collection and analyses.</span></div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><div><b>Sensitivity</b> </div><div>Are small changes reflected in the results? <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Changes in the component of care being measured should be captured by the </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">measurement process and reflected in the results. The performance indicator should be </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">capable of detecting changes in the quality of care and these changes must be reflected in </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">the resulting values. </span></div><div><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></div><div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b>Specificity</b> </span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Does the KPI actually capture changes that occur in the service for</span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">which the measure is intended? </span></span><span>Only changes in the area being measured are reflected in the measurement results</span></div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><div><b>Relevance</b> </div><div>What useful decisions can be made from the KPI? <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The results of the measurement should be of use in planning and the subsequent delivery </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">of healthcare and contribute to performance improvement</span></div><div><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></div><div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b>Balance</b> </span></span></div><div><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Do we have a set of KPIs that measure different aspects of the </span></span><span>service? </span><span>The final suite of indicators should measure different aspects of the service in order to </span><span>provide a comprehensive picture of performance, including user perspective</span></div><div><span><br /></span></div><div><span></span><br /><div><span><b>Tested</b> </span></div><span><div>Have national and international KPIs been considered? <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">There should be due consideration given to indicators that have been tried and tested in </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">the national and international arena rather than developing new indicators for the same </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">purpose.</span></div><div><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></div><div><b>Safe</b> </div><div>Will an undue focus on the KPI lead to potential adverse effects on <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">other aspects of quality and safety? </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The indicator should not lead to an undue focus on the aspect of care being measured that </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">may in turn lead to a compromise in the quality and safety of other aspects of the service.</span></div><div><br /></div><div><b>Avoid duplication</b> </div><div>Has consideration been given to other projects or initiatives? <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Prior to developing the indicator due consideration should be given to other projects or </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">initiatives to ensure that there will not be a duplication of data collection.</span></div><div><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></div><div><b>Timeliness</b></div><div>Is the information available within an acceptable period of time to <span style="font-size:18.6667px;">inform decision-makers? </span><span process. If the data is required for operational </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">purposes, then it will be required within a shorter timeframe than data used for long term </span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">strategic purposes. </span></div></span></div></div></div></div></div></div></div><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">METADATA:</span></span></h1><span>Data that defines and describes other data</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">MINIMUM DATA SET:</span></span></h1><span>The minimum set of data elements that are required to be collected for a specific purpose</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">NUMERATOR:</span></span></h1><span>The specifications that define the subset of data items in the denominator that meet the indicator criteria.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">KEY PERFORMANCE INDICATORS:</span></span></h1><span>Performance Indicators are specific and measurable elements of practice that can be used to assess quality of care. Indicators are quantitative measures of structures, processes or outcomes that may be correlated with the quality of care delivered by the healthcare system. </span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">PROCESS INDICATORS:</span></span></h1><span>Performance indicators that monitor the activities carried out in the assessment/diagnosis and treatment of service users.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">OUTCOME INDICATORS:</span></span></h1><span>Performance indicators that monitor the desired states resulting from care processes, which may include reduction in morbidity and mortality, and improvement in the quality of life.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">RELIABILITY:</span></span></h1><span>Reliability is the consistency of your measurement, or the degree to which an instrument measures the same way each time it is used under the same condition with the same subjects.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">STRUCTURE INDICATORS:</span></span></h1><span>Performance indicators that monitor the attributes of the health system that contribute to its ability to meet the healthcare needs of the population.</span><br /><span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">The Delphi Technique:</span></span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The Delphi technique is a facilitated structured process whereby a panel of experts </span></span><span>complete questionnaires (see Appendix 3 for example) remotely and, through feedback </span><span>and scoring over a number of rounds where some KPIs are discarded, a consensus is </span><span>achieved on a final set of KPIs. The panel need not ever meet face-to-face and each </span><span>individual’s feedback is provided anonymously to the panel, which eliminates the </span><span>possibility of undue influence by dominant personalities within the panel.</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">The RAND appropriateness method:</span></span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The RAND appropriateness method combines scientific evidence with expert</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">opinion by facilitating experts to rate, discuss and re-rate KPIs.
