<div style="text-align:justify;"><span><i style="color:rgba(0,0,0,0.75);">Pretty much every article starts with the challenges that pharmaceutical industry across the globe is facing. It is a difficult industry and everybody acknowledges that, considering the time to develop an original drug (10-15 years), the costs involved (last time I checked it was USD 2-3 billion), the high attrition rates of drug candidates (1 out of 5,000 or 10,000 leads make way for FDA approval), the tough regulatory environment which is varied across countries and geographies, and the rising pressures on pricing (pricing advantage for truly outcome-driven therapeutics). All of these, with the looming patent expiry, the imminent entry of generics, and the tantalizing RoIs, make it even more difficult.</i></span></div>

Well, technology is here to rescue. It will be a grave injustice to talk about technology implementation in pharma R&amp;D in just one article, and because AI and ML are the current buzzwords, I thought, maybe, we could specifically discuss the role&nbsp;<span style="background:0 0;border:0;box-sizing:inherit;font-weight:700;margin:0;outline:0;padding:0;vertical-align:baseline;">Artificial Intelligence&nbsp;</span>plays in streamlining and improving success rates of pharma R&amp;D. Before we go any further, let us have a quick look at the drug discovery process and the typical timelines associated.
<div class="separator" style="clear:both;text-align:center;"><a href="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/b6879-manishreepharma1.jpeg" style="margin-left:1em;margin-right:1em;"><img border="0" height="168" src="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/adb15-manishreepharma1.jpeg" width="640"></a></div>
</div>
<div class="slate-resizable-image-embed slate-image-embed__resize-full-width"></div>
<div>More often than not, it takes more than a decade (sometimes &gt; 15 years) for a new drug to enter the market. One should not forget if technologies and in-silico modeling are not effectively used in early stages of drug discovery, drug failure at a later stage is a significant waste of time and money. Now, pharma companies have already been using computational tools to conduct ADMET predictions and in-silico modeling, so what new has Artificial Intelligence to offer?</div>
<div>To answer this question, important will be to look at some of the AI initiatives and activities of key pharmaceutical companies. You may find initiatives where pharma companies are scouting for AI innovation via their open innovation program, so if you are a tech start-up, think about it.
<div class="separator" style="clear:both;text-align:center;"><a href="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/08fd2-manishreepharma2.jpeg" style="margin-left:1em;margin-right:1em;"><img border="0" height="532" src="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/b45db-manishreepharma2.jpeg" width="640"></a></div>
</div>
<div class="slate-resizable-image-embed slate-image-embed__resize-full-width"></div>
<div>The list is not exhaustive and is only for the purpose of illustration, but one can clearly see that leading pharma companies have already dipped their toes in Artificial Intelligence-mediated drug discovery and development.</div>
<div><span style="background:0 0;border:0;box-sizing:inherit;font-weight:700;margin:0;outline:0;padding:0;vertical-align:baseline;"><em>Going back to the drug discovery process diagram, let us superimpose the AI-applications across the process.</em></span>

<div class="slate-resizable-image-embed slate-image-embed__resize-full-width"></div>
<div>Artificial Intelligence and Machine Learning algorithms, not only simplify the existing tasks/processes, it saves time, while adding significant value. Let us understand this better.</div>
<div><span style="background:0 0;border:0;box-sizing:inherit;font-weight:700;margin:0;outline:0;padding:0;vertical-align:baseline;">BioXcel Corporation</span>, a biopharmaceutical company is working on integrating big data and AI into drug discovery process. One such product is EvolverAI which uses AI algorithms for drug discovery to find the best therapy, thus reducing drug failure. Evolver AI uses big data to screen through huge volumes of structured and unstructured data related to genes, proteins, disease pathways, targets, symptoms etc. within the field of Neuroscience. This is followed by creation of meta-data, which contains network-maps, linking pathophysiology of diseases with drugs. These meta-data are then fed into a decision matrix, which compares all the drugs. Using AI algorithms and human intelligence, several hypotheses are built based on the known linkages, of which the best hypothesis gets selected for future experiments and clinical trials, significantly reducing both time and risk of drug failures.
<div class="separator" style="clear:both;text-align:center;"><a href="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/56bf7-manishreepharma4.jpeg" style="margin-left:1em;margin-right:1em;"><img border="0" height="430" src="https://hcitexpert.com/wp-content/uploads/2020/02/a965d-manishreepharma4.jpeg" width="640"></a></div>
</div>
<div class="slate-resizable-image-embed slate-image-embed__resize-full-width"></div>
<div><span style="background:0 0;border:0;box-sizing:inherit;font-weight:700;margin:0;outline:0;padding:0;vertical-align:baseline;">Exscientia</span>, which has partnerships with both GSK and Sanofi, uses AI to learn best practices from drug discovery data, and helps researchers generate drug candidates in much lesser time.</div>
<div>The beauty of such algorithms is in their ability to go though various data-sets – from&nbsp;<span style="background:0 0;border:0;box-sizing:inherit;font-weight:700;margin:0;outline:0;padding:0;vertical-align:baseline;">medical records, publications, clinical trial data, available data on disease pathways and drug-disease correlation</span>&nbsp;to derive meaningful analysis that can help researchers in decision making. Similarly, screening through EHR, current and past clinical trials, and available publications on epidemiology, can help in site selection for clinical trials. Applications are many, and this is still an evolving area, with many developments happening in the stealth mode.</div>
<div>Many diseases such as&nbsp;<span style="background:0 0;border:0;box-sizing:inherit;font-weight:700;margin:0;outline:0;padding:0;vertical-align:baseline;">cancers and neurodegenerative disorders</span>, having high mortality and morbidity, have been debilitating for people across the globe, with pharma companies pouring in significant amount of money, yet with not-so-significant success rates.</div>
<div><span style="background:0 0;border:0;box-sizing:inherit;font-weight:700;margin:0;outline:0;padding:0;vertical-align:baseline;">Take Alzheimer’s for example</span>, after three large clinical trials for solanezumab, which was called the breakthrough drug candidate, it hasn’t been able to display significant change in patient’s conditions as compared to a placebo.&nbsp;Solanezumab is an anti-Aβ mAbs, that targets Amyloid beta, which is one of the main components of the amyloid plaques found in the brains of Alzheimer’s patients. Does that mean researchers are looking in the wrong place? Is there a need to rethink the disease pathway and establish newer biomarkers to be targeted? THESE are the areas where AI and ML can help.</div>
<div>Similarly, increasingly the drug research community is realizing that not all drugs work on all patients. AI algorithms can help stratify patient population to identify what causes drug efficacy in some patients while nothing really in others - could be due to an aberration, mutation, any specific biomarker – and AI can help recognize the same in the most efficient manner possible.</div>
<div>And that is not all, there is a lot more - we are just scratching the surface, but one cannot deny that these technologies are the next hope for pharmaceutical companies. What do you think?</div>
