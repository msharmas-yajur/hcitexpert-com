style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;text-align:justify;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">ADDRESSING THE KNOWLEDGE GAP THROUGH CDSS:</span></span></h1><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;text-align:justify;"><span>THE POWER OF ORDER SETS</span></h1><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">A “Physician Order” is a communication directing a particular service or action </span></span><span>to be taken in the care of a specific patient. Medications, diet, physical activities, </span><span>laboratory tests, radiologic studies, therapies, treatments...all are among the </span><span>literally dozens of orders written to guide the care of each and every patient by </span><span>the physician throughout an ordinary day. </span><br /><span></span><br /><a name='more'></a><span><br /></span> <span>Thus the physician ordering process is </span><span>complex and time-consuming. In addition, the continuous explosion of new </span><span>evidence-based information results in the reality that providers often make </span><span>mistakes, at best failing to provide the highest value care, and at worst causing </span><span>preventable injuries and deaths. And while computers can address avoidable </span><span>mistakes from the most mundane sources (such as illegible hand-writing), the </span><span>greatest threat to patient safety and cost waste is the knowledge gap.</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Fortunately, when a physician realises that he or she needs information, CDSS </span></span><span>reference solutions provide access to current, credible, evidence-based knowledge </span><span>(either integrated into an EHR, available over the internet, or in print). Thus by </span><span>their very nature, reference solutions require that the physician knows he or she </span><span>doesn’t know something.</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">But medical knowledge is doubling every two months. Clearly many times the </span></span><span>physician doesn’t know what he or she doesn’t know... Thus patients are placed </span><span>at risk because physicians are unaware that new information and knowledge is </span><span>available.</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Order sets are the best solution to this dangerous problem. Order sets </span></span><span>automatically push current, credible, evidence-based information specific to the </span><span>patient’s clinical history and current clinical status directly to the physician at the </span><span>point of care. Take for example:</span><br /><span><br /></span> <br /><blockquote class="tr_bq"><span style="color:#a64d79;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">A 52 year old man is admitted for surgical treatment of a right-sided </span></span><span>colon cancer. His surgeon regularly operates on such patients, </span><span>removing that segment of large intestine harboring the malignant </span><span>tumor. But like many, this surgeon is unaware that this patient’s young </span><span>age and tumor location suggest an inherited syndrome requiring a </span><span>much more extensive operation to prevent a second cancer over the </span><span>next decade.</span></span> </blockquote><blockquote class="tr_bq"><span style="color:#a64d79;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">If the surgeon “doesn’t know what he doesn’t know,” how can he look </span></span><span>up “inherited colon cancer” in his CDSS reference solution? He can’t. </span><span>But when the patient is admitted to the hospital, order sets specific </span><span>for colon cancer patients are automatically pushed to the physician. </span><span>These order sets can be commercially available or can be created by the </span><span>hospital, healthcare system, regional, or international experts </span><span>(physicians, nurses, pharmacists, etc.) and represent the evidence-based </span><span>guidelines and information on colon cancer. Thus the order sets </span><span>educate the surgeon and recommend that he order a simple blood </span><span>test to check for the inherited cancer syndrome. If integrated within an </span><span>EHR, the physician can actually click on embedded hyperlinks to view </span><span>the EBM sources of the recommended orders.</span></span> </blockquote><blockquote class="tr_bq"><span style="color:#a64d79;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The surgeon will likely accept the recommended order and confirm that </span></span><span>the patient suffers from the syndrome. Then the surgeon can search </span><span>the CDSS reference solution and rapidly learn the appropriate surgical </span><span>procedure for the patient, as well as how to test and screen family </span><span>members for the inherited syndrome.</span></span></blockquote><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Thus order sets address the knowledge gap, including providing the physician with </span></span><span>what he “doesn’t know he doesn’t know.”</span><br /><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">But there is a risk with evidence-based order sets because clinical knowledge is </span></span><span>advancing exponentially. When order sets are implemented but inadequately </span><span>maintained, they drive providers to practice outdated medicine on a widespread </span><span>basis [14]. Thus it is critical for evidence-based order sets to include a knowledge-base </span><span>that continually reflects current evidence. In the near future, evidence-adaptive </span><span>order sets will be empowered through advancements in machine learning and </span><span>artificial intelligence. </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Today, much evidence adaption is performed manually, with </span><span>professionals (using computer systems) to rapidly review new EBM for updating </span><span>order sets. CDSS which incorporate order sets can reduce medication errors up </span><span>to 81% [15], and today, order sets represent the most impactful CDSS solution to </span><span>empower physicians in delivering the highest quality, most cost-efficient </span><span>evidence-based patient care.</span><br /><span></span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">THE ECONOMIC ARGUMENT FOR ORDER SETS</span></span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">One of the greatest challenges of healthcare reform worldwide is the reluctance </span></span><span>of those paying for technology to invest in EBM and CDSS. The question, of </span><span>course, is return on investment (ROI). However, the potential ROI of order sets </span><span>through reduction in adverse drug events (ADE) and unnecessary diagnostic </span><span>tests alone is projected to be enormous (in one academic hospital estimated at </span><span>up to $10 million [16]). Although there remains a dearth of high-quality evidence on </span><span>the cost impact of order sets, many operational benefits which intuitively link to </span><span>cost reduction have been demonstrated. Including: reductions in overall length </span><span>of stay; postoperative length of stay; and the total cost for multiple surgical </span><span>procedures, including total knee arthroplasty, appendectomy, total laryngectomy, </span><span>cholecystectomy, carotid endarterectomy, gastrectomy, inguinal hernia repair, </span><span>and colon surgery [17].</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">University of Kentucky Healthcare (UKHC) adopted a well-known commercial order </span></span><span>sets solution in 2013 [18], demonstrating improvements in compliance to standard </span><span>practices and elimination of unnecessary tests. At the University Hospital Frankfurt </span><span>in Germany, implementation of order sets focused on gastroenterologic care </span><span>reduced average length of stay and overall physician ordering time while elevating </span><span>physician satisfaction scores for computerised ordering [19].</span><br /><span></span><br /><h1 style="color:black;font-family:Times;font-style:normal;font-variant-caps:normal;font-variant-ligatures:normal;letter-spacing:normal;line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;orphans:2;text-align:justify;text-decoration-color:initial;text-decoration-style:initial;text-indent:0;text-transform:none;white-space:normal;widows:2;word-spacing:0;"><span>ORDERING BETTER CARE: CONCLUSION</span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The multi-factorial healthcare dilemma including preventable medical errors, </span></span><span>the information explosion, slow knowledge diffusion, a growing regulatory </span><span>environment, and increasing litigation has rendered Clinical Decision Support </span><span>Systems indispensable. </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Order sets are designed not only to answer questions that </span><span>the physician is asking, but also to answer critical questions that the physician </span><span>doesn’t know he or she should be asking. Founded in current, credible, </span><span>evidence-based information, order sets are the most impactful of physician CDSS </span><span>solutions. </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Combined with reference and other CDSS solutions, order sets have </span><span>the potential to empower physicians in providing the safest, highest quality, most </span><span>cost-efficient healthcare; that is, a truly reliable Poka-Yoke.</span><br /><span><span style="color:#ff5e0e;font-size:24px;font-weight:bold;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:24px;"><b>Suggested Reading</b></span></span><br /><br /><span>Dr. Ujjwal was also asked in a recent interview with BioSpectrum India, to share more about the challenges, and most urgent needs in today’s healthcare systems. </span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Some might argue that technology is the way forward but Dr. Ujjwal is of the view that technology is only the vehicle through which information and knowledge is delivered.
