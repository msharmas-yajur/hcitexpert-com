Here's the gist!</i></span></blockquote><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-2a068dbc-fe6e-999c-4605-22a11f0a3f6b" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><hr /></div><div dir="ltr" style="margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><div style="line-height:2.0736;"><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;text-align:justify;"><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.295;margin-bottom:8pt;margin-top:0;text-align:justify;"><span>Before I delve any deeper, let’s understand what ROI is.</span></div><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;text-align:justify;"><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;text-size-adjust:auto;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">ROI can mean different things to different people. To nurses and infection control teams, ROI means ‘Risk of Infection’. To most of us burdened by home loans, car loans and education loans, ROI means 'Rate of Interest’. To the CEO who makes gut-wrenching investments and wants to make money back, ROI means ‘Return on Investment’. As for me, the emergency physician in me wants to take ROI at its face value, but <b>the clinical informaticist in me thinks of ROI as the ‘Radius of Information’</b>.</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"></span></span><br /><a name='more'></a><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Let me illustrate what this means. Think about the “radius of information” as a measure of <b>the circle of knowledge, i.e. the super-set of all that we know in medicine </b>(Fig 1). From all this knowledge, there are things that you know and things that you don't know i.e., you know what you know and you know what you don't know. However, with an explosion in the rate of medical information growth coupled with the slow adoption of research findings into clinical practice, <b>more often than not, physicians don't know what they don't know</b>. This is what is known as knowledge variability. No two doctors, no two nurses have the same amount of knowledge, and that is at the core of knowledge-based medical errors.</span></span></div></div><span> </span><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;text-align:center;"><span><span style="background-color:transparent;color:#262626;font-size:11pt;font-style:normal;font-variant:normal;font-weight:400;text-decoration:none;vertical-align:baseline;white-space:pre;"><img height="426" src="https://lh5.googleusercontent.com/pWrk9caUEhKvcphs6IZCnefaJ8PiMcYfDt0JroBFVwfXfpypwkcDAuGVlS41fmaL0slDdAfgh-UfrktbrddpqRpnkdUXeb8smWze2KE9odGntJHg-pLGc8RuFimmHLX03e5zrk3gmqLW6i2dUA" style="-webkit-transform:rotate(0.00rad);border:none;transform:rotate(0.00rad);" width="624" /></span><span style="background-color:transparent;color:#262626;font-size:11pt;font-style:normal;font-variant:normal;font-weight:700;text-decoration:none;vertical-align:baseline;white-space:pre;">Figure 1</span></span></div><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.295;margin-bottom:8pt;margin-top:0;"><span> </span></div><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;"><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;"><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;text-size-adjust:auto;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The other fundamental cause of medical errors is operational variability. Operational variability arises, for example, when a physician’s handwriting results in a prescription error. Similarly, there are many categories of operational lapses that result in medical errors.</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Reducing operational variability is usually the mainstay of most quality programs and health IT is often deployed to yield returns from operational optimization. But, the question remains, <b>what about knowledge-based errors</b>, where care providers at best fail to provide high-value care and, at worst, cause preventable medical errors and deaths due their knowledge gaps? <b>Knowledge variability is hardly ever addressed by health IT</b>.</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><b>Knowledge variability stems from the “Knowledge Dilemma”</b> (Fig 2) posed by two determinants of medical knowledge – Diffusion Time and Doubling Time. On one hand, it takes, on an average, 17 years for research to move into day-to-day clinical practice and on the other hand, medical knowledge will soon double every 73 days!</span></div></div><br /></div><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;text-align:center;"><span><img height="389" src="https://lh6.googleusercontent.com/GKrlj9wD-uEeJOXHGoOlacEEQFSKXj3cgXis-UPADOhmbmLan4si7Z-rAkJpmSXSivHOihUzRsoD8rkVyv2Wo3PJizdXJSFb-JQGoWsPiFIGZUXOQpyp5n_HdsINyr8bcD16x-otB4FXb2XxAQ" style="-webkit-transform:rotate(0.00rad);border:none;transform:rotate(0.00rad);" width="624" /></span></div><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;text-align:center;"><span>Figure 2</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:5pt;margin-top:5pt;"><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.2;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;"><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;text-size-adjust:auto;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Now, <b>with this disastrous collision of realities, is the practice of medicine no longer “humanly” possible?</b> How can we avoid variability in patient care while keeping current? And how can we optimize ROI of health IT?</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">In order to truly address new challenges appearing as our entire healthcare delivery model evolves, <b>technology needs to be evidence-adaptive</b> i.e., to have a knowledge-base that continually reflects current evidence so that it can bring the right information to physicians at the right time, at the point-of-care. <b>Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS) are the most pertinent answer to the vast and destructive problem of variability in care delivery</b>. Evidence-adaptive CDSS from authoritative sources have proven to reduce spending on unnecessary tests and procedures as well as avoid costly adverse events (and in many systems, malpractice litigation claims). Although at present evidence-adaptive platforms require human intervention, we are now beginning to see the inclusion of artificial neural networks (deep learning), Bayesian networks, reinforcement learning, and other <b>artificial intelligence techniques for synthesizing evidence relevant to patient data in real-time, with potentially unprecedented insights for clinicians.</b></span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><b>Intelligence Augmentation (IA)</b> with evidence-adaptive CDSS, where technology amplifies the decision-making capabilities of humans, links healthcare providers to vast amounts of patient data in the Electronic Health Record (EHR) with relevant clinical knowledge, in real-time, at the point-of-care. This can <b>dramatically increase clinicians’ ROI – radius of information</b>, thereby improving clinical outcomes and <b>consequently increasing the healthcare organization’s ROI – return on investment</b>. Indeed, a longer ROI for a higher ROI!</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">End note: Wondering if you should build evidence-adaptive CDSS in-house or opt for a third-party solution?
