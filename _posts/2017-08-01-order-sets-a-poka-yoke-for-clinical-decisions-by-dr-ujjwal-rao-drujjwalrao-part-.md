Why?</span></b></i></div></blockquote><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-2a068dbc-fe6e-999c-4605-22a11f0a3f6b" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><hr /></div><div dir="ltr" style="margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><div style="line-height:2.0736;"><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-7f8576b5-ff00-784f-b097-8486d6015797" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:10pt;text-align:justify;"><span>FIRST, DO NO HARM</span></div><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:1.8;margin-bottom:10pt;margin-top:6pt;text-align:justify;"><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The Supreme Court of India recently ordered one of the largest </span></span><span>compensations so far in the country to a girl who lost her vision at birth in </span><span>a case of medical negligence. The girl, who is now 18 years old, was born </span><span>prematurely at a government hospital but was discharged from the hospital </span><span>without a retinopathy test, a must for prematurely born babies. By the </span><span>time the family discovered the lapse, the girl had lost her vision [1].</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"></span></span><br /><a name='more'></a><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Fentanyl is a potent opioid medication used as part of anesthesia. A hospital </span></span><span>pharmacist received an order for a ‘fentanyl drip 5,200 mcg per hour,’ which a </span><span>nurse had just transcribed after accepting a telephone order. The pharmacist </span><span>called the nurse to clarify the dose. The nurse confirmed that, although the </span><span>dose was large, she had “read back” the order to the anesthesiologist several </span><span>times to make sure she had heard the dose correctly. The pharmacist called </span><span>the anesthesiologist himself, only to find that the intended order was for a </span><span>fentanyl drip 50 to 100 mcg per hour [2].</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">The frequency of preventable medical errors resulting in patient injury </span></span><span>and death is staggering. It is estimated that for every 100 hospitalisations, </span><span>approximately 14 adverse events occur, translating to roughly 43 million </span><span>avoidable patient injuries worldwide each year. In terms of quality of life for </span><span>those inadvertently hurt: the loss of nearly 23 million years of healthy life [3]. </span><span>And avoidable medical errors don’t just injure patients. Between 200,000 and </span><span>400,000 patients die every year in the United States as a result of preventable </span><span>medical errors, [4] making avoidable hospital deaths the number three killer of </span><span>American adults. </span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">These stunning figures clearly directly oppose the fundamental principle of </span></span><span>medicine: First, Do No Harm.</span><br /><span></span> <br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span>THE MEDICAL INFORMATION EXPLOSION</span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Based on an extrapolation of a 2011 study [5] the stacking of CD-ROMs </span></span><span>holding all of medical information available by 2020 would reach from earth to </span><span>the moon and a half of the same distance beyond. And the rate of our medical </span><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">knowledge growth is hard to fathom: <b><i>by 2020, all that humanity understands </i></b></span></span><b><i>about the body, health, and healthcare is projected to double every 73 days</i></b><span> [6].</span><br /><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Just to keep up with the Primary Care literature would require a General </span></span><span>Practitioner to read for 21 hours every single day [7]!</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span>DIFFUSION OF KNOWLEDGE TAKES (A LONG) TIME</span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">“Diffusion of medical knowledge” is the acceptance of new scientific </span></span><span>discoveries into clinical practice. And such diffusion takes an extraordinarily </span><span>long time... </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Back in the early 19th century, the idea of hand washing prior to examining </span><span>pregnant women was considered revolutionary, and it was only after decades </span><span>that hand washing to prevent puerperal fever was universally accepted in </span><span>clinical practice. But you don’t have to look so far back. Take the case of </span><span>β-blockers, a class of drugs whose beneficial effect in heart attack patients </span><span>was established almost 30 years ago. Yet today, β-blockers are still widely </span><span>under-prescribed [8]. </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>The tragic reality is that even today, it takes an average of 17 years for only 14% </span><span>of new scientific discoveries to find their way into daily clinical practice [9]. Thus </span><span>our patients routinely wait to be prescribed drugs or undergo procedures or </span><span>interventions proven effective decades earlier.</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">In the end, we have a disastrous collision of realities: all medical knowledge </span></span><span>will soon be doubling every 73 days, while it will likely take decades for any </span><span>new knowledge to routinely be incorporated into patient care.</span><br /><span></span> <br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span>GOOD CARE PAYS - POOR CARE COSTS</span></h1><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Healthcare is being reformed globally. In particular, the payment models are </span></span><span>increasingly moving away from Fee-for-Service (FFS) to Pay-for-Performance </span><span>(P4P). Full-fledged or partial P4P models are now increasingly being adopted by </span><span>most of the developed nations, including the USA, UK, and Australia, among </span><span>others. P4P models aim to encourage care providers (individuals and institutions) </span><span>to provide better quality care by linking reimbursement (provider payments) to </span><span>clinical and performance outcomes. The models also penalise medical errors, adverse </span><span>outcomes, and excessive diagnostic and treatment costs. Thus in the P4P model, </span><span>providers and healthcare systems risk significant financial penalties if they are </span><span>unable to avoid adverse clinical outcomes and unnecessary tests and procedures.</span><br /><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">To summarise, healthcare is now faced with a new dilemma: a significant </span></span><span>burden of preventable medical errors, an explosion in the rate of medical </span><span>information growth, and the historically slow adoption of new discoveries. </span><span>Add to this an expanding regulatory environment demanding high-quality </span><span>care plus the rapid rise of medical malpractice litigation and providers must ask </span><span>themselves, “Is the practice of medicine no longer humanly possible?”</span><br /><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span><span style="font-size:24px;">A SOLUTION TO THE MULTI-FACTORIAL</span></span></h1><h1 style="line-height:1.56;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:24pt;"><span>HEALTHCARE DILEMMA</span></h1><span><br /></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">So how do we reduce (and eventually eliminate) preventable medical errors? </span></span><span>Providing current, credible, evidence-based information and guidance at all points </span><span>of care is a cornerstone in the answer to this question. In the area of medication </span><span>errors (a common form of preventable patient injury and death), a system analysis </span><span>of a large sample of serious mistakes [10] identified 16 major types of causative </span><span>system failures. All of the top eight were deemed preventable through the </span><span>provision of better medical information.</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Today, Clinical Decision Support Systems (CDSS) are being hailed as a major</span></span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">weapon in the battle against preventable medical errors [11]. And at the heart of </span></span><span>the most impactful CDSS lies evidence-based medicine (EBM). Advocated as a </span><span>method to improve clinical outcomes [12], the incorporation of EBM into powerful </span><span>CDSS has the potential to transform healthcare safety and quality, a true </span><span>healthcare Poka-Yoke! As such, EBM is the foundation of evidence-based care, </span><span>broadly defined as patient management through the conscientious and judicious </span><span>use of current best evidence from clinical care research integrated with </span><span>individual clinical expertise [13]. And to complete the picture, evidence-based care </span><span>should also include patient preferences, input, and active participation. </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Clearly based on the foundations of the healthcare dilemma, in order to be safe, </span><span>effective, and efficient, today’s physicians, nurses, pharmacists, therapists, patients, </span><span>and other healthcare stakeholders must have real-time, mobile access to </span><span>current, credible, evidence-based information. While many have been disappointed </span><span>that Electronic Health Records (EHRs) have not on their own solved the dilemma, </span><span>it is critical to appreciate that technology is the vehicle through which EBM and </span><span>other information is delivered, not the primary source of information itself. In </span><span>the absence of technology (in fact, long prior to the development of computers </span><span>and the internet), current, credible, evidence-based information allowed the </span><span>world’s leading healthcare providers to deliver high quality, evidence-based care.</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Today’s technology represents a great leap forward in accessing high value care </span></span><span>information at points across the globe, with the knowledge provided by EBM </span><span>integrated into EHRs and available via “the cloud,” all as part of CDSS.</span><br /><span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;"><br /></span></span> <span><span style="font-size:18.6667px;">Evidence-based care is most impactful when current, credible, evidence-based </span></span><span>knowledge is incorporated into the provider workflow; thus, the most advanced </span><span>CDSS are “workflow-integrated.” More importantly, these systems are </span><span>evidence-adaptive [12]; that is, the clinical knowledge within the CDSS continually </span><span>reflects current EBM from the research literature plus sources of practice </span><span>expertise. </span><br /><span><br /></span> <br /><blockquote class="tr_bq"><span><i>The full potential of a CDSS can be realised when it is seamlessly </i></span><span><i>integrated into the clinical workflow and is evidence-adaptive [12]</i>.</span></blockquote><span><b>Stay tuned for the Part TWO of the Blog from Dr. Ujjwal Rao.</b></span><br /><span><b><br /></b></span> <span><span style="color:#ff5e0e;font-size:24px;font-weight:bold;">Suggested Reading</span></span><br /><span>Dr. Ujjwal was also asked in a recent interview with BioSpectrum India, to share more about the challenges, and most urgent needs in today’s healthcare systems. </span><br /><span><br /></span> <span>Some might argue that technology is the way forward but Dr. Ujjwal is of the view that technology is only the vehicle through which information and knowledge is delivered.
