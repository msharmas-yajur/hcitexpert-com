style="clear:both;text-align:center;"></div><h2 id="docs-internal-guid-63a53c91-f639-1422-5eba-a71e1b3c4f1a" style="line-height:1.728;margin-bottom:0;margin-left:36pt;margin-top:0;text-align:justify;"><div dir="ltr" id="docs-internal-guid-2a068dbc-fe8a-2577-b355-686d962ca276" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><div class="separator" style="clear:both;text-align:center;"></div><div class="separator" style="clear:both;text-align:center;"></div><span><br /></span><span>The Health Statistics play a major role in deciding the Health Policies of Nations, no doubt they provide the insights into the health parameters in question and health statistics such as Rates, Ratio, Incidence, Prevalence and Life Tables are needed to be formed into indicators of progress of the nation in terms of improvement of the factors in consideration.</span></div><a name='more'></a><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>The question is – is mere statistics enough or is there something more to it?</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>As we move from Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) to Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) – this question becomes more imperative!</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>According to WHO, Health is defined as “Health is a state of complete physical, mental and social well-being and not merely the absence of disease or infirmity”. 1</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Health is also identified the state of “not well”, “ill “or “morbidity” or “sickness”. Further “death”, which is caused by illness or sickness, reflects the condition of Health. Hence measurement of “Mortality” and “Morbidity” reveals of Health condition of a community.2</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Statistics definitely provide a macro-level / micro-level understanding of the situation and brings in quantitative pointer to a qualitative, exhaustive and comprehensive activity. Year-on-year the quantitative pointer is kept as the reference. Any deviation from it is representative of the growth or decline of the parameters in consideration.</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Such laser sharp focus on quantitative data is the hallmark for Population Census, Surveys and even Market Research – where absolute number, a figure in percentage is the gospel truth.</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>What about Health / Medical Data of the population? What about the Medical History? What about the Medical Condition – improvement / degradation?</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>The last point gets easily converted into an explainable number or a percentage as an indicator, but what about the relapse or repeat condition of the same person – it’s again… just a number!  </span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Limitations of data especially in developing countries are a real concern, as available data is not reliable and post-2015 presents an opportunity to think beyond what data is available so that countries can invest in capacity building to get it.3</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Alongside the number comes a lot of health and health-related data, but with the focus being statistics, basic essential health data is obviously missed out.</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Non-communicable disease</span><span> continues to be an important public health problem in India, being responsible for a major proportion of mortality and morbidity. Surveillance of NCDs and their risk factors should also become an integral function of health systems. Evidence based clinical practice and appropriate use of technologies should be promoted at all levels of health care, including tertiary services.4</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>With the focus on Non-Communicable diseases (NCDs) – it is imperative we focus on the Healthcare &amp; Medical Data for Clinical outcomes and not mere Health Information for Management Systems.</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Ageing Population</span><span> is one of the major concern globally, more so in India. It is now recognized that while both developed and developing countries are experiencing growing proportions of elderly, developing countries currently are ageing faster than developed countries. In India, the proportion of the population aged 60 years and above was 7 per cent in 2009 (88 million) and is expected to increase to 20 per cent (315 million) by the year 2050. 7</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>As per a study conducted 5, among the most significant findings that emerged was the incompleteness of data on the burdens of access and affordability among elderly populations in India. A major reason for this is that routine health data collection in India is not designed to reflect or characterize pathological progression. Many routine data collection procedures (National Sample Surveys, Census data, or death certificates) in India do not capture pathological progression nor do they disaggregate morbidity and disability outcomes among the elderly. 5</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Further research, especially qualitative research, is needed to explore the depth of the problems of the elderly. 6</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>With a rise in the Ageing Population, it is all the more important to look at the efficacy of the health data collected than mere health statistic data and a Longitudinal Study along with Cross-Sectional Study is needed for an efficient health data repository.</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>With the advent of new age technology: connected technology, Connected Health has become pervasive and with embedded systems and IoT taking center-stage, it is all the more essential to focus on the Data and not mere Number! And when I say ‘Data’ it is ‘Health Data’.</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>And last, but not the lease – the necessity for increased clinical / medical research in today’s evidence based approach makes it is all the more important to focus on the fundamental health data collection, collation, transformation and consolidation to begin with leading to Analysis, Research and building the knowledge base for Healthcare / Medical Data Management.</span></div><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span>Looking at the underlining need for Health / Healthcare Data, we need to move from just Statistical Data to Meaningful Data – not to mistake ‘Meaningful use of Data’!</span><br /><span><br /></span><span style="font-family:pt serif;font-size:18.6667px;vertical-align:baseline;"><i>The article was first published on Mr. Atul Bengeri's - LinkedIn Pulse. The article is republished here with the authors' permission</i></span></div></h2><h2 id="docs-internal-guid-63a53c91-f639-1422-5eba-a71e1b3c4f1a" style="line-height:1.728;margin-bottom:0;margin-left:36pt;margin-top:0;text-align:justify;"><br /></h2><br /><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;"><span><span style="font-size:large;">References</span></span></div><ol style="margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;"><li><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:6pt;text-align:left;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;vertical-align:baseline;">World Health Organization (WHO) <a href="http://www.who.int/about/definition/en/print.html">http://www.who.int/about/definition/en/print.html</a></span></div></li><li><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;text-align:left;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;vertical-align:baseline;">Manual on Health Statistics in India <a href="http://mospi.nic.in/Mospi_New/upload/Manual-Health-Statistics_5june15.pdf">http://mospi.nic.in/Mospi_New/upload/Manual-Health-Statistics_5june15.pdf</a></span></div></li><li><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;vertical-align:baseline;">CIGI, TISS, KDI, Post-2015 Development Goals, Targets and Indicators: Indian Perspectives, Mumbai, India / Meeting Report, August, 2012</span></div></li><li><div dir="ltr" style="line-height:2.0736;margin-bottom:0;margin-top:0;"><span style="font-size:18.6667px;vertical-align:baseline;">An Overview of the Burden of Non- Communicable Diseases in India – R Prakash Upadhyay.
