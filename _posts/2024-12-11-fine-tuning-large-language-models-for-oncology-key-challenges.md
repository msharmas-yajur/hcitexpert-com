<h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>1.The role of Data Centric AI in Health Systems</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p id="ember2266">In the rapidly evolving landscape of artificial intelligence applications, particularly in healthcare, the data-centric approach to AI model development has become increasingly crucial. However, the shift toward data-centric AI necessitates a fundamental reimagining of these systems to now enable a focus across the entire care continuum of the citizen, as also the need to link the Real World Data to the Real World evidence systems, after all one relies on the other for generating workflows and insights.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p id="ember2267">Modern healthcare infrastructure must evolve to accommodate data centric workflows, moving beyond conventional data management to supporting not only advanced analytics but should now include data centric-Ai capabilities. <h2 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>2. Recommendations</strong></h2>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong><em>2.1 Time-Stamped Data Pipeline</em></strong></h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Clinical Knowledge has the following contexts; patient context, clinical protocol context and research context. Each of these contexts have an important temporal element that should be incorporated into any framework that develops the workflows to prepare the data for LLM learning context. For instance, Newer research publications mandate the need to Implement a system where oncology data is time-stamped to track when knowledge was added or updated. Time-stamped clinical protocols data (NCCN, etc) needs to be identified and added to this framework to ensure tracking updates. Finally, the Patient-specific data undergoes the fastest updates, capturing the temporal component to the patient data will help in the context window to be clearly defined for episodic or visit contexts. And finally patient matching data for similar patients based on some real world evidence criteria to keep track of the common protocols being followed by the clinicians which will then allow them to further tweak the treatment protocols specific for each patient.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Implementation Example: Develop a data ingestion system that automatically tags each piece of information with its publication date and last update date. This could be integrated with major oncology databases and journals to ensure real-time updating. A similar mechanism will be implemented for each of the three types of data to ensure a recent and relevant update of information.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Potential Drawback: The volume of time-stamped data could become overwhelming, necessitating efficient storage and retrieval systems. This can be circumvented by ensuring keeping track timestamped data for the data pipeline at the time of data ingestion.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong><em>2.2 Regular Ingestion of Various Data Sources</em></strong></h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Clinical Guidelines Resources are constantly being updated. There is a need to develop a process that Continuously updates the LLM with the latest clinical guidelines from leading oncology organizations like the NCCN, ASCO, and ESMO websites, downloading and processing new guidelines as they are published. Similarly, clinical protocols and patient information will be made ready for data ingestion whenever there are changes. Each data type will be made available for data ingestion to ensure recent and relevant information is always available for the LLM for an updated context window.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Potential Drawback: Guidelines may sometimes conflict across organizations, requiring a system to manage and reconcile differences by enabling a maker-checker workflow to enable the clinicians to validate the guidelines. Mitigating factors can be enabled by incorporating various methodologies to enable versioning for various data sources. The end-user will have the ability to refer specific data sources to train the models in training or in production.&nbsp;</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p></p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong><em>2.3 Clinical Trial and FDA Approval Data</em></strong></h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Provide data from ongoing and completed clinical trials, as well as new FDA approvals for cancer drugs and treatments.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Implementation Example: Develop an API connection to ClinicalTrials.gov and the FDA database to automatically ingest new trial data and drug approvals. To filter the sheer volume of clinical trial data it will be necessary to implement sophisticated filtering mechanisms to identify the most relevant information for the context for which the LLM is being trained. For instance, filtering of information based on the tumor type.&nbsp;In addition to these initial data ingestion excercise, there needs to be additional workflows needed to be integrated to enable the creation of patient cohorts and most importantly the inclusion and exclusion criterias. In addition, there needs to be an integration to the onboarding workflows that will allow an early detection mechanism to onboard patients in a more seamless manner with a clear view of the patient history.</p>

<!-- wp:heading {"level":3} -->
<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong><em>2.4 Active Learning for Continuous Updating</em></strong></h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Active learning focuses on improving model performance by iteratively selecting and incorporating the most informative data into the training set. For oncology, where research evolves rapidly, active learning ensures that models remain current and relevant by:</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Incorporating new insights from cutting-edge publications.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Addressing gaps or biases in existing datasets.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Enhancing model robustness and accuracy.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":4} -->
<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Key Components of the Implementation</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":5} -->
<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>a. Data Collection and Selection</strong></h5>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Mechanism</strong>: A system is designed to scan databases like PubMed, arXiv, and oncology-specific repositories for high-impact publications.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Flagging Criteria</strong>:<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Citation count or citation velocity.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Keywords or topics matching critical oncology advancements (e.g., “immunotherapy,” “genomic biomarkers”).</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Endorsements by oncology experts or institutions.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list --></li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Preprocessing</strong>: Extract relevant content (e.g., abstracts, results) and transform it into a structured format for model training.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":5} -->
<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>b. Continuous Model Retraining</strong></h5>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Automated Integration</strong>: Integrate flagged publications into the training pipeline with minimal manual intervention.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Retraining Workflow</strong>:<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Pre-train on the updated dataset.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Validate the updated model using oncology-specific benchmarks.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Deploy only after passing robustness tests.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list --></li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Retraining Frequency</strong>: Define intervals (e.g., quarterly or semi-annually) based on the volume of new data and computational resources.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Implement an active learning mechanism where the model periodically retrains on new oncology data.</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Implementation Example: Design a system that flags new, high-impact oncology publications and automatically incorporates them into the model's training data, triggering regular retraining cycles. Refer <strong><em>Appendix A</em></strong> for more detailed information on implementing active learning for LLMs</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>Potential Drawback: Frequent retraining could lead to model instability if not carefully managed.</p>

<h3 class="wp-block-heading"><strong><em>2.5 Version Control on Oncology Datasets</em></strong></h3>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:paragraph -->
<p>In oncology, treatments and research evolve rapidly. Version control enables systematic tracking of changes in datasets, offering:</p>
<!-- /wp:paragraph -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Historical Insights</strong>: Analyze how protocols or outcomes have changed over time.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Data Integrity</strong>: Maintain a clear lineage of modifications for reproducibility.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Comparative Analysis</strong>: Easily compare old and new data to identify trends or validate results.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<hr class="wp-block-separator has-alpha-channel-opacity"/>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Key Components of Version Control Implementation</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":5} -->
<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>a. Dataset Organization</strong></h5>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Structure</strong>: Organize datasets into modular components based on:<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Cancer type (e.g., breast, lung, colorectal).</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Data modality (e.g., imaging, genomics, clinical notes).</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Time periods (e.g., pre-2020 vs. post-2020).</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list --></li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Standardized Formats</strong>: Use interoperable formats like FHIR - JSON, openEHR or specialized medical standards (e.g., SNOMED, DICOM for imaging).</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":5} -->
<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>b. Versioning Mechanism</strong></h5>

<!-- wp:list-item -->

<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Annotations (e.g., corrections in data interpretation).</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Source (e.g., datasets derived from TCGA, SEER, or clinical trials).</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>
<!-- /wp:list --></li>
<!-- /wp:list-item --></ul>

<h4 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>Potential Challenges and Solutions</strong></h4>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:heading {"level":5} -->
<h5 class="wp-block-heading"><strong>a. Computational Costs</strong></h5>
<!-- /wp:heading -->

<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Challenge</strong>: Managing and storing multiple versions of large oncology datasets (e.g., genomic or imaging data) can be resource-intensive.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li><strong>Solution</strong>:<!-- wp:list -->
<ul class="wp-block-list"><!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Use delta storage techniques: Store only the differences between versions rather than entire datasets.</li>
<!-- /wp:list-item -->

<!-- wp:list-item -->
<li>Leverage cloud-based storage solutions with auto-scaling capabilities.</li>
